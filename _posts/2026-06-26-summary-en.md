## [First complete Herculaneum scroll read using AI](https://scrollprize.org/firstscroll) ⭐️ 9.0/10

Researchers have successfully read an entire Herculaneum scroll for the first time, using AI-based ink detection and digital unwrapping techniques developed through the Vesuvius Challenge. This achievement marks a historic breakthrough in digital archaeology, enabling the recovery of ancient texts previously considered unreadable. It could unlock hundreds more scrolls buried at Herculaneum, potentially rediscovering lost works of classical philosophy and literature. The team used machine learning models trained to detect carbon-based ink on micro-CT scans of the rolled scrolls, segmenting the virtual layers to read continuous text. The method avoids physically unrolling the brittle carbonized papyrus, which often destroys the scroll.

hackernews · verditelabs · Jun 25, 15:48 · [Discussion](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48675179)

**Background**: The Herculaneum papyri were carbonized by the eruption of Mount Vesuvius in 79 AD and are too fragile to unroll. The Vesuvius Challenge, launched in 2023, offers prizes for automated reading of the scrolls using AI. Previous attempts using X-ray imaging and ink detection had only recovered isolated words or phrases.

## [German Court Holds Google Liable for AI Overview Errors](https://simonwillison.net/2026/Jun/25/ai-and-liability/#atom-everything) ⭐️ 9.0/10

A German court ruled that Google is liable for errors in its AI-generated overviews, treating them as Google's own words. Bruce Schneier argues that AI agents should be considered agents of the organization that deploys them. This landmark decision sets a precedent for AI liability, meaning companies cannot shield themselves from responsibility by blaming AI errors. It could transform how businesses deploy AI in critical areas like legal, medical, and customer service. The ruling specifically applies to Google's AI overviews, which are generated by large language models. The legal principle invoked is analogous to vicarious liability, where employers are liable for employees' actions, extending it to AI systems.

rss · Simon Willison · Jun 25, 22:28

**Background**: Vicarious liability is a legal doctrine that holds employers responsible for wrongs committed by employees in the course of employment. This German ruling applies that concept to AI, meaning that a company deploying AI is legally accountable for its outputs, just as if it had hired a human to do the work. The decision is groundbreaking because it directly addresses the question of who is responsible when AI produces inaccurate or harmful content.

## [Compiling Agentic Workflows into LLM Weights](https://www.reddit.com/r/MachineLearning/comments/1ufgpnh/r_compiling_agentic_workflows_into_llm_weights/) ⭐️ 9.0/10

A new paper proposes compiling agentic workflows into LLM weights via supervised fine-tuning on traces from frontier models, achieving near-frontier quality at two orders of magnitude less cost. This breakthrough could enable cost-effective deployment of high-quality AI agents by using small language models fine-tuned on traces from expensive frontier models, significantly reducing inference costs. The method compiles persistent procedural knowledge into model weights, while keeping transient state in the prompt, analogous to traditional compilation. It leverages supervised fine-tuning on traces from agent orchestration frameworks like LangGraph and CrewAI.

reddit · r/MachineLearning · /u/ThirdWaveCat · Jun 25, 17:31

**Background**: Agentic workflows involve multiple interdependent LLM calls, often leading to high token costs and latency. Small language models (SLMs) are cheaper but less capable. This paper shows that SLMs can be fine-tuned on execution traces from stronger models to internalize the workflow logic, drastically reducing cost while maintaining performance.

## [Hybrid Model Token Prediction Analysis](https://huggingface.co/blog/allenai/hybrid-token-prediction) ⭐️ 7.0/10

Researchers at AI2 released a blog post analyzing which tokens hybrid models (combining autoregressive and masked language modeling) predict better compared to pure autoregressive or masked models. This analysis helps NLP researchers understand the strengths of hybrid architectures, guiding model design for tasks where specific token types are critical, such as named entities or rare words. The study likely compares token-level prediction accuracies across different token categories (e.g., common words, rare words, punctuation) and provides insights into when hybrid models outperform pure alternatives.

rss · Hugging Face Blog · Jun 25, 16:11

**Background**: Hybrid models like MARIA (Masked and Autoregressive Infilling Architecture) integrate autoregressive (AR) next-token prediction with masked language modeling (MLM) fill-in-the-blank objectives. AR models excel at open-ended generation, while MLM models are better at understanding context bidirectionally. This blog explores which tokens benefit most from the combination.
